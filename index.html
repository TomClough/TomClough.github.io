Otago Field School in Ocean Beach

Ocean Beach in Hawkes Bay is an area densly populated with archaeological sites yet very few have been the subject of archaeological investigations.

In 2021 the University of Otago, with the generous agreement of local iwi and landowners, was able obtain consent to conduct an archaeological investigation and train provide training to archaeology students.

The field shcool took place over three weeks where three archaeological sites were investigated spanning the length of Ocean Beach consisting of deflated middens and early ocupation layers located within the dune systems and the investigation of the Matarau Pa site.

This website is not designed as a report of what was found at the 2021 Otago field shcool.

This is an attemp to infom people about the types of techniques that Archaeologists in the field use as a means to learn about the past. What is shown on this page is a sample of archaeological techniques that were used by the students of the field school

In the field there activities fall within four categories: Survey, Excavation, Sampling, and Recording

Survey

It turns out that a large part of archaeological survey is walking. To get a good understanding of the local environment and to identify possible archaeological areas you need to spend alot of time examining every bit of ground and every eroding bank.

You can then use test pits to try identify or confirm the extents of any subsurface archaeological material.

Drone surveys are also excellent at identifying and photographing eroded or excavated features.

However the most important part of a survey is knowing what others have already recorded which can be found on ArchSite

Excavation

Exvavation is potentially the most exciting part of archaeology and is what generates the most data.

Excavation can be done in various differnt ways and with differnt tool, from digging a trench by hand to using a mechanical excavator. However, on this field school we focussed on three main hand excavation techniques. Exposing eroding sections, trenching, and areal excavations.

The main formula that was followed during this field school revoleved around setting up a string line prior to digging and splitting each area into quatrants or sections. From there each section was evenly taken down and with material sieved and sampled. When a layer was reached with visible archaeological features they were excavated and recorded.

It is during excavation where you get to find interesting artifacts such as bone spears, fish hooks, and adzes

Sampling and Recording

Sampling and recording are the most important part of any archaeological field work (but not the most fun). It is also an ongoing process that conintues throuhout and after the excavation phase.

If an archaeological investigation is conducted but no samples and records are taken, then there may aswell have been no investigation in the first place. An Archaeologists job is to record the past and if they do no then they are not an Archaeologist.

During the Field School samples were collected during excavation with each sample baged and labled with what area and what section and layer it came from. These samples included shell, charcoal, and artifacts. For the areas where no excavation was possible such as the deflated middens, samples were collected from material lying on the surface along their length.

Each excavation area was recorded with detailed notes on features, cultural materials, and soils, with additional scale drawings to show stratigraphy (layers) and measurements.
